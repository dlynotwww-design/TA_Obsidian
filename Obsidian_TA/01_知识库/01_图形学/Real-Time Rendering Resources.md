Two chapters and two appendices are available *only* online, for free:

- **[Collision Detection](https://www.realtimerendering.com/Real-Time_Rendering_4th-Collision_Detection.pdf)** - The 3rd edition's chapter on this subject has been fully updated, but could not fit in the bounds of the physical book, so we offer it here, along with its [hyperlinked bibliography](https://www.realtimerendering.com/refs.html#colldet).
- **[Real-Time Ray Tracing](https://www.realtimerendering.com/Real-Time_Rendering_4th-Real-Time_Ray_Tracing.pdf)** - API support for this area was [announced in March 2018](http://www.realtimerendering.com/blog/ray-tracing-at-gdc/), too late for inclusion in the book, so we wrote an additional chapter about the subject as a whole. Its [hyperlinked bibliography](https://www.realtimerendering.com/refs.html#raytracing) is available. Better still, you can access this chapter directly [here](https://github.com/tcoppex/Real-Time-Rendering-4th-ch26) *(thanks, Thibault Coppex!)*.
- **[Appendices](https://www.realtimerendering.com/Real-Time_Rendering_4th-Appendices.pdf)** - The two appendices, *Some Linear Algebra* and *Trigonometry*, also had to be cut from the physical book. They have both been updated (a small bit) from the 3rd edition. The [hyperlinked bibliography](https://www.realtimerendering.com/refs.html#appendices) is also available.

## Introduction

Our book is something of a practitioner's manual; it assume some basic knowledge of the field. For the basics, and not so basics, you may want to start at [Scratchapixel](https://www.scratchapixel.com/) (you can skip some of the Mathematics sections to start). For a video introduction to computer graphics from the ground up, see [Cem Yuksel's lectures](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLplnkTzzqsZTfYh4UbhLGpI5kGd5oW_Hh) at the University of Utah.

How to pronounce all these graphics terms? [Here's a guide](http://casual-effects.blogspot.com/2016/03/computational-graphics-pronunciation.html). Remember, "SIGGRAPH" rhymes with "pig laugh". For [Bézier, Gouraud, Fresnel,"](http://www.realtimerendering.com/blog/bzier-gouraud-fresnel/) listen [here](https://www.realtimerendering.com/BezierGouraudFresnel.mov).

There is not much else related to the Introduction of the book to put here, so we'll list some free graphics books that may be of interest. See our **[graphics book list](https://www.realtimerendering.com/books.html)** for upcoming, recent, and recommended books.

These are books that are **FREE ONLINE**, ordered by publication date. Do not be fooled by the price; all but one were published as physical books and each has valuable information.

## The Graphics Rendering Pipeline

Commercial game engines include: [Unity](https://unity3d.com/), [Unreal engine](https://www.unrealengine.com/), and [CryEngine](https://www.cryengine.com/), to name a few. Find a [overflowing table of engines](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_game_engines) on Wikipedia.

[Irrlicht](http://irrlicht.sourceforge.net/), [bgfx](https://github.com/bkaradzic/bgfx), [OGRE](http://ogre.sourceforge.net/), [Godot](https://godotengine.org/), and [sauerbraten](http://sourceforge.net/projects/sauerbraten) are open source 3D games engines with some popularity. ## Transforms

[Immersive Linear Algebra](http://immersivemath.com/ila/index.html) is a free interactive book on linear algebra, coauthored by Tomas. [Dave Eberly's site](http://www.geometrictools.com/) has useful papers and code on a wide variety of geometric operations, including quaternion interpolation. [J.M.P. van Waveren](http://mrelusive.com/publications/papers/SIMD-From-Quaternion-to-Matrix-and-Back.pdf) discusses matrix to quaternion transforms and how to use SIMD for them. [Code](http://fileadmin.cs.lth.se/cs/Personal/Tomas_Akenine-Moller/code/) for rotating from one vector to another rapidly using quaternions (as described in our book) is available online from Tomas.

The [Portable Game Library](http://plib.sourceforge.net/) includes code for a [Simple Geometry library](http://plib.sourceforge.net/sg/index.html).

Dual quaternion skinning offers improved quality at relatively little additional cost. The process can be [done using a vertex shader](http://chinedufn.com/dual-quaternion-shader-explained/). An old-but-free [Maya plugin](http://mayadqskinning.sourceforge.net/) is also available.

The [Graphics Gems](http://graphicsgems.org/) book series contains a number of articles on transformations, with code online.

As we touch upon in our book, moving your z-buffer's near plane as far from the eye as possible is a good idea. Steve Baker has an [article on this topic](http://www.sjbaker.org/steve/omniv/love_your_z_buffer.html) with a little calculator to explore the effect. Nathan Reed [discusses options to improve precision in depth](https://developer.nvidia.com/content/depth-precision-visualized) (we use one of his graphs in our book). An older article [gives other visualizations of precision problems](http://outerra.blogspot.com/2012/11/maximizing-depth-buffer-range-and.html). Using a [reversed z-buffer](https://nlguillemot.wordpress.com/2016/12/07/reversed-z-in-opengl/) is one solution - this article also links to other articles on the subject.

It's possible to perform [skinning in a compute shader](https://turanszkij.wordpress.com/2017/09/09/skinning-in-compute-shader/amp/).

## Shading Basics

We drew upon Tarek Sherif's [WebGL 2 Examples](https://github.com/tsherif/webgl2examples) for our shader examples. This code is an easy way to start playing with shaders.

There are many shader tutorials out there. For example, [Unity 3D](https://unity3d.com/learn/tutorials/topics/graphics/gentle-introduction-shaders) and [Learn OpenGL](https://learnopengl.com/Getting-started/Shaders) provide basics, [Ronja](https://www.ronja-tutorials.com/) gives some more elaborate effects. [The Book of Shaders](https://thebookofshaders.com/) is an introduction to fragment shaders, under development. [Shadertoy](https://www.shadertoy.com/) is at the far end of the spectrum, an interactive web environment doing everything in the fragment shader; [Omar Shehata](https://gamedevelopment.tutsplus.com/tutorials/a-beginners-guide-to-coding-graphics-shaders--cms-23313) provides an introduction. Other web-based systems for experimentation include [GLSL Sandbox](http://glslsandbox.com/) (similar to Shadertoy), [Shdr](http://shdr.bkcore.com/) for basic inputs and meshes to play with, [Shadershop](http://tobyschachman.com/Shadershop/) for visualizing shader functions, and [ISF](https://www.interactiveshaderformat.com/) for video DJs.

Dealing with the explosion of shaders caused by material systems is an ongoing concern. [This article](https://dolphin-emu.org/blog/2017/07/30/ubershaders/) discusses the problem in depth and offers solutions.

Storing semitransparent textures so that the colors are premultiplied by the alphas makes compositing and blending operations much faster to compute. Tom Forsyth gives a rundown of the [math and formats involved](https://tomforsyth1000.github.io/blog.wiki.html) (search "Premultiplied alpha part 2").

Transparency is difficult to perform correctly in a single pass when using a Z-buffer. Steve Baker gives a [good summary](http://www.sjbaker.org/steve/omniv/alpha_sorting.html) of the basics of the problem and traditional solutions. [NVIDIA's developer site](http://developer.download.nvidia.com/SDK/10/direct3d/samples.html) and the [Humus 3D site](http://www.humus.name/index.php?page=3D) each have sample code for using stencil routing to provide order-independent transparency. Tarek Sherif made a [WebGL 2 browser demo of depth peeled transparency](https://tsherif.github.io/webgl2examples/oit-dual-depth-peeling.html).

NVIDIA provides a good summary of [basic techniques for transparency](https://developer.nvidia.com/content/transparency-or-translucency-rendering) and some common optimizations. This article at Sketchfab gives [an artist's view of transparency](https://blog.sketchfab.com/real-time-transparency-make-fast-beautiful/) and using blending modes to simulate it.

The dangers of improperly accounting for alpha in cutouts is [lavishly illustrated in this blog post](http://www.adriancourreges.com/blog/2017/05/09/beware-of-transparent-pixels/).

24 bits of color is usually enough, but not always. Here's an [image showing concentric bands](https://www.realtimerendering.com/cubes.gif) only one pixel intensity value apart. Most monitors will show some banding somewhere on the image.

Here is a comparison generated using [Scott R. Nelson's](http://www.tandfonline.com/toc/ujgt19/2/1) program of lines drawn with [gamma=1.0](https://www.realtimerendering.com/gamma10.png) (note the severe roping and Moíre patterns) and properly drawn with [gamma=2.2](https://www.realtimerendering.com/gamma22.png). I find this pretty convincing.

Larry Gritz's article [The Importance of Being Linear](https://developer.nvidia.com/gpugems/GPUGems3/gpugems3_ch24.html) is an excellent explanation of gamma correction and other monitor transforms. If that article doesn't click for you, maybe [this one](http://filmicworlds.com/blog/linear-space-lighting-i-e-gamma/) will.

The difference between linear, sRGB, and gamma 2.2 are [shown in this Shadertoy](https://www.shadertoy.com/view/lsd3zN).

The chart on [this page](https://web.archive.org/web/20181121192841/https://techreport.com/review/12458/amd-radeon-hd-2900-xt-graphics-processor/6) shows different MSAA patterns used on various vendors' GPUs. Lots more on [sampling pattern theory here](https://cs.dartmouth.edu/~wjarosz/publications/subr16fourier-slides-2-patterns.pdf). This [FXAA vs. SMAA](http://international.download.nvidia.com/geforce-com/international/comparisons/watch-dogs/watch-dogs-anti-aliasing-comparison-1-smaa-vs-fxaa.html) interactive demo lets you examine the differences. Matt Pettineo's blog post describes the [problems with MLAA approaches under animation](https://mynameismjp.wordpress.com/2012/10/24/msaa-overview/). The [retrospective here](http://www.highperformancegraphics.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/Retrospective/HPG2017_Jimenez_MLAARetrospective.pdf) gives a thorough history of the use of MLAA techniques in games.

This [article on alpha coverage](https://medium.com/@bgolus/anti-aliased-alpha-test-the-esoteric-alpha-to-coverage-8b177335ae4f) includes some excellent animations and figures showing various problems and solutions.

Font antialiasing via sub-pixel LCD rendering is dealt with in depth on the [Anti-Grain Geometry site](https://web.archive.org/web/20190326231019/http://www.antigrain.com:80/research/font_rasterization/index.html). GPU-accelerated 2D engines include [Will Dobbie's](http://wdobbie.com/post/gpu-text-rendering-with-vector-textures/) (try the [*War and Peace* demo](http://wdobbie.com/warandpeace/), YMMV).

This [thorough survey](http://behindthepixels.io/assets/files/TemporalAA.pdf) of temporal antialiasing (TAA) techniques is from 2020. Code that demonstrates the MSAA and temporal antialiasing techniques used in "The Order: 1886" can [be found here](https://github.com/TheRealMJP/MSAAFilter). The [demos here](https://blog.sketchfab.com/introducing-temporal-anti-aliasing/) are not truly temporal antialiasing, but rather progressive antialiasing, and still worth a look to see the improvement over time.

Newer techniques continue to be developed. [DLSS](https://www.eurogamer.net/articles/digitalfoundry-2018-dlss-turing-tech-analysis), deep-learning super-sampling, is as it sounds, using deep learning training to create a neural net that improves an aliased image.

## Polygonal Techniques

[MeshLab](https://www.meshlab.net/) is an open source system for manipulating meshes. It has a huge number of meshing operations available. The [Meshlab blog](http://meshlabstuff.blogspot.com/) has worthwhile articles, including a [rundown of experiments performed](http://meshlabstuff.blogspot.com/2009/04/on-computation-of-vertex-normals.html) comparing three different vertex normal computation techniques.

Gavin Bell describes a bit more about how to get the [normals to point outwards](http://www.realtimerendering.com/resources/RTNews/html/rtnv7n5.html#art16), along with sample code.

[GTS](http://gts.sourceforge.net/) is an (ancient) open-source, LGPL polygonal manipulation library that does VIPM, stripification, hierarchical bounding box generation, and more.

The [Virtual Terrain Project](http://www.vterrain.org/) has a huge amount of useful information about terrain storage and rendering, [large terrain datasets](http://vterrain.org/BT/index.html), as well as source code.

## Pipeline Optimization

We list a number of tools for debugging in the book, here are the links:

- [RenderDoc](https://renderdoc.org/) - a high-quality Windows, Linux, and Android debugger for DirectX, OpenGL, and Vulkan, originally developed by Crytek and now open source.
- [AMD's GPU Analyzer](https://gpuopen.com/rga/) - AMD's suite of tools for their graphics hardware offerings, working on Windows and Linux.
- [NVIDIA Nsight](http://www.nvidia.com/object/nsight.html) - a performance and debugging system with a wide range of features. It integrates with Visual Studio on Windows and Eclipse on Mac OS and Linux.

- [Graphics Performance Analyzers (GPA)](https://software.intel.com/en-us/gpa) - a suite from Intel, not specific to their graphics chips, that focuses on performance and frame analysis.

- [Metal System Trace](https://crimild.wordpress.com/2016/06/16/praise-the-metal-part-7-profiling-and-optimizing-our-app/) - provides tracing information from the application, driver, and GPU for Metal applications.

## Collision Detection

The [Physics Simulation Forum](http://www.bulletphysics.com/Bullet/phpBB3/) has many threads about collision detection and physical simulation.

One related hardware product is [NVIDIA's PhysX processor](http://www.nvidia.com/object/nvidia_physx.html) (they purchased Ageia), a dedicated physics action accelerator.

A number of free collision detection packages are available on the Web. These include source, and most have limitations on commercial reuse:

- [Bullet Physics Library](http://bulletphysics.org/wordpress/) - library for performing rigid-body collision detection and response. Open source and free for commercial use, and is integrated with Blender.
- [SOLID](https://github.com/dtecta/solid3) - Software Library for Interference Detection. Now a commercial product, and GPL'ed with source.
- [ColDet](https://sourceforge.net/projects/coldet/) - a free collision detection library for generic polyhedra.
- [Havok](http://www.havok.com/) - a popular commercial library for games

Related to collision detection, [Qhull](http://www.qhull.org/) implements the Quickhull algorithm for finding convex hulls quickly. The [Stony Brook Algorithm Repository](http://www3.cs.stonybrook.edu/~algorith/) has convex hull and other code in its computational geometry section.

## Linear Algebra

The interactive [Immersive Linear Algebra](http://immersivemath.com/ila/index.html) book is a great way to build up your intuition on the geometric interpretation of various operators and elements.

Flipcode has a [3D geometry primer](http://www.flipcode.com/geometry/) online.

[Wolfram MathWorld](http://mathworld.wolfram.com/) is an incredible resource for (sometimes dense) mathematical definitions. You can find a collection of math-related definitions at [Cut the Knot](http://www.cut-the-knot.org/glossary/atop.shtml).

Christer Ericson has a nice presentation on the [scalar triple product](http://realtimecollisiondetection.net/blog/?p=13), a way to compare the orientation of one line compared to another. He also has a [followup article](http://realtimecollisiondetection.net/blog/?p=69).

[Maxima](http://sourceforge.net/projects/maxima/) is a symbolic computation program, like Mathematica and Maple: you define equations and can easily combine them, integrate, take the derivative, etc. Maxima is GNU source now, and free.
